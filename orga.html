Introduction to Economics

Fundamental concepts, typologies & economic history

💡

What is Economics?

▼
Definition Economics is the science that studies how individuals and organizations use limited resources to satisfy multiple needs. It analyzes choices made when resources (time, money, technology) are scarce.

Etymology: From Ancient Greek Oïkos (house/estate) + Nomos (rule/management) → "the management of the household".

Why Economics for Engineers? The engineer is not just a technician — they are a resource manager. They must ensure market fit (viability), make buy vs. build decisions (CAPEX/OPEX), and understand the market context before launching a project.
🔑

Key Economic Concepts

▼

Scarcity

Resources are limited while needs are unlimited. This forces choices and trade-offs.

Economic Agent

Any individual or entity that makes economic decisions: households, firms, government, rest of the world.

Microeconomics

Studies individual agents — a firm's pricing decision, a student choosing between laptops.

Macroeconomics

Studies economy-wide phenomena — inflation, unemployment, GDP, exchange rates.

CAPEX

Capital expenditure — one-time investments (buying a server). Creates fixed assets.

OPEX

Operational expenditure — recurring costs (cloud subscription). Flexibility without ownership.
⚙️

The Three Fundamental Economic Activities

▼
ActivityDescriptionIT Example
ProductionCreation of goods/services using factors of productionDeveloping a software app
ConsumptionUse of goods/services to satisfy needsBuying a SaaS subscription
Capital FormationInvestment to grow productive capacityBuying servers, hiring engineers
Value Added (VA) VA = Revenue − Intermediate Consumption (IC). IC includes cloud hosting, electricity, raw materials — costs consumed in production. Profit = VA − Wages − Taxes − Depreciation.
🌱

Economic Trends

▼
  • Circular Economy: Minimize waste by keeping resources in use (repair, reuse, recycle IT equipment)
  • Digital Economy: Value creation through data, platforms, and software (scalability is key)
  • Inclusive Economy: Economic activity that benefits all groups, reducing inequality
  • Globalization: Integration of markets — local companies compete globally; multinationals adapt locally
Scalability The ability of a system or business to grow without proportional increase in cost. Central to the digital economy — software can serve 10 or 10 million users at near-zero marginal cost.
🔄

Economic Circuit & Flows

▼

Real Flows

Movement of goods, services, and labor between agents. Example: a worker provides labor to a firm.

Monetary Flows

Counter-flow of money. The firm pays wages in exchange for the worker's labor.

In the simple circuit: Households supply labor → firms pay wages → households spend on goods → firms receive revenue. The circular economy tries to recover value at end-of-life (e-waste recycling) rather than destroy it.

Organization Overview

Concepts, types, stakeholders & historical theories

📖

Defining an Organization

▼
Robbins & Mathew (1990) "An organization is a consciously coordinated social entity, with a relatively identifiable boundary, that functions on a relatively continuous basis to achieve a common goal or set of goals."
Richard L. Daft (2012) Organizations are (1) social entities, (2) goal-directed, (3) deliberately structured activity systems, (4) linked to the external environment.
H. Mintzberg (1982) "An organization can be defined as the sum total of the ways in which labour is divided into distinct tasks and then its coordination is achieved among these tasks."
Simple Summary An organization is a group of people where work is divided into different tasks, and these tasks are coordinated to achieve common goals.
🎯

Main Missions of an Organization

▼
Economic Social Public Service Financial Innovation Environmental

Organizations combine resources (human, financial, material) to achieve specific objectives. Their missions may be single or multiple — a company can simultaneously be economic and social.

🏢

▼

Organizational Systemic Perspective

Internal & external environments, systems theory

⚡

What is a System?

▼
Definition A system is an organized set of interdependent elements that interact with each other to achieve a common objective.
ElementFor a Firm
InputsCapital, labor, information, raw materials
ProcessProduction, management, coordination
OutputsGoods, services, economic value
EnvironmentMarket, regulations, competitors

Key Idea: A company is an open system — it continuously interacts with its external environment. Its performance results from a combination of strategic, human, and organizational factors, not technology alone.

🏠

Internal Environment

▼
  • Organizational Structure: How tasks and departments are arranged (functional, divisional, matrix, hierarchical)
  • Leadership Style: Democratic (consults team) · Authoritarian (decides alone) · Transformational (inspires change) · Laissez-faire
  • Organizational Culture: Shared values, beliefs, and behaviors that define how people work together
  • Motivation: Salary, bonuses, recognition, advancement opportunities, training
  • Communication: Internal flow of information — good communication improves coordination and reduces conflict
Structure TypeDescriptionBest For
FunctionalOrganized by department (HR, Finance, Marketing)Stable, single-product companies
DivisionalOrganized by product, region, or customerMultinationals, diverse product lines
MatrixDual authority — employees have 2 managersConsulting firms, project-based work
HierarchicalClear vertical chain of commandMilitary, traditional bureaucracies
🌍

External Environment — PESTEL

▼

The PESTEL framework analyzes macro-environmental forces affecting the organization:

FactorExamples
🏛️ PoliticalGovernment stability, trade policy, tax law
💰 EconomicInflation, unemployment, exchange rates, GDP growth
👥 SocialDemographic trends, cultural attitudes, consumer behavior
💻 TechnologicalAI, automation, digital infrastructure, R&D investment
⚖️ EnvironmentalCO₂ regulations, sustainability requirements, climate risk
📜 LegalData protection laws, labor law, competition law
Opportunities vs. Threats External factors can be either opportunities (new markets, tech breakthroughs, rising purchasing power) or threats (new competitors, inflation, new regulations). Strategic decision-making requires continuous environmental monitoring.

Functions of an Organization

Operational & support functions, their roles and interactions

🗺️

Overview of Organizational Functions

▼
Function In a company, functions correspond to groups of tasks that contribute to the same result. They work together in a complementary way to achieve the organization's goals.
FunctionRole
📣 Sales / MarketingGenerate revenue; manage product, price, place, promotion (4Ps)
🏭 ProductionTransform raw materials into finished goods
🛒 PurchasingNegotiate and source raw materials and supplies
🚚 LogisticsTransport, store, and distribute goods
📦 Inventory MgmtMaintain optimal stock levels
💼 Finance / AccountingManage budgets, invoices, salaries, profitability
👤 Human ResourcesRecruit, train, motivate, and manage employees
🔬 R&DDevelop new products, technologies, and innovations
📊 ManagementPlan, organize, lead, control all activities
📣

Sales Function — The 6 Steps

▼

The sales process follows a structured 6-step approach:

  • Step 1 — Prospecting: Identify and contact potential customers
  • Step 2 — Qualification: Evaluate whether the prospect can become a satisfied customer
  • Step 3 — Sales Presentation: Demonstrate your product/service as the best solution
  • Step 4 — Handling Objections: Prepare effective responses to prospect resistance
  • Step 5 — Closing: Secure the sale and convert prospect to customer
  • Step 6 — Follow-up / Loyalty: Maintain satisfaction and generate repeat sales
The 4Ps of Marketing Product (what to produce?) · Price (at what price?) · Place (where to sell?) · Promotion (how to increase sales?)
🏭

Production Function

▼
Objective Transformation of raw material flows and information flows into finished products.

Push Production

Make-to-stock: produce for inventory based on demand forecasts. Efficient for high-volume, standardized goods.

Pull Production

Make-to-order: produce only when a customer order is received. Reduces waste; ideal for customized products.

The strategic choice between push and pull depends on market conditions, product type, and desired service level.

🔬

R&D Function

▼
  • Develops new products and services
  • Tests and validates new technologies
  • Develops new applications and software
  • Analyzes market trends and competitive environment
  • Drives long-term competitive advantage through innovation
Importance R&D is the engine of innovation. For digital startups, R&D investments in fixing bugs, improving UX, and adding AI features directly determine customer satisfaction and market position.

Exercise 0.1 — The "Best Code in the World" Case

▼

Context: A team of brilliant engineers creates an ultra-secure messaging app — flawless code, zero bugs. After 2 years of development, they realize that the server cost per message is 10 DA, but users are only willing to pay 2 DA per subscription. The company goes bankrupt in 3 months.

Q1: Is this a technical or economic failure?

Q2: What economic question should you have asked before writing the first line of code?

✅ Model Answer

Q1: This is an economic failure. The technology was perfect, but the business model was unviable. The cost to deliver the service (10 DA) exceeded what customers were willing to pay (2 DA), making every transaction a loss.

Q2: The key economic question was: "What is the maximum price the market is willing to pay, and can we deliver at a lower cost?" More specifically: "What is our break-even point?" This is a question of market fit and unit economics — core economic concepts that must precede technical development.

Exercise 0.2 — Make or Buy Decision (CAPEX vs OPEX)

▼

Option A (Make): Develop chat in-house. Takes 2 months with 2 engineers at 100,000 DA/month each.

Option B (Buy): Integrate existing SaaS solution at 20,000 DA/month.

Q1: What is the total cost of Option A?

Q2: How many months of Option B does Option A cost?

Q3: What economic concept is illustrated by the opportunity cost of engineer time?

✅ Model Answer

Q1: Option A Cost = 2 engineers × 100,000 DA/month × 2 months = 400,000 DA

Q2: 400,000 DA ÷ 20,000 DA/month = 20 months of Option B

Q3: This illustrates Opportunity Cost — the value of the best alternative foregone. By having engineers build the chat, you lose 2 months of work on your core product. The true cost of "Make" includes what you give up by NOT doing something else.

Exercise 0.3 — The Phantom Invoice (Scarcity & Cost Management)

▼

A student trains an AI model on a cloud GPU server at 200 DA/hour. Training takes 2 hours, but the student forgets to shut it down and leaves for the weekend (48 hours).

Q1: What is the useful cost? What is the wasted cost?

Q2: What lesson do you draw about technical vs. management competence?

✅ Model Answer

Q1: Useful (productive) cost = 2 hours × 200 DA = 400 DA
Wasted (unproductive) cost = 48 hours × 200 DA = 9,600 DA
Total cost = 10,000 DA — 96% wasted!

Q2: Technical competence (knowing how to launch a server) is not sufficient alone. Management competence — knowing how to monitor, control, and manage resources/budgets — is equally critical. An engineer who cannot manage costs is a liability, not just an asset.

Exercise 5 — Macroeconomics & Real vs. Nominal Salary

▼

You are hired as a junior engineer at 80,000 DA in 2023. In 2024, salary raised to 84,000 DA. The ONS reports an inflation rate of 8% for 2024.

Q1: Has your purchasing power increased, stayed the same, or decreased?

Q2: What does this show about the importance of macroeconomics for your career?

✅ Model Answer

Q1: Nominal salary increase = (84,000 − 80,000) / 80,000 = 5%
Inflation = 8%
Real wage change ≈ 5% − 8% = −3%
→ Your purchasing power has DECREASED by ~3%, even though your nominal salary increased. You earn more money but can buy less.

Q2: Understanding macroeconomics is essential for salary negotiations and career planning. A raise below the inflation rate is effectively a pay cut. Engineers who understand macroeconomic indicators can make better financial and career decisions.

Exercise 6 — Value Added Calculation (Dzaïr-Soft)

▼

Startup "Dzaïr-Soft" 2023 data:

Revenue: 5,000,000 DA · PC depreciation: 500,000 DA · Cloud hosting: 200,000 DA · Electricity/Internet: 100,000 DA · Salaries: 3,000,000 DA · Office supplies: 50,000 DA

Q1: Calculate Intermediate Consumption (IC).

Q2: Calculate Value Added (VA).

Q3: Calculate Profit.

✅ Model Answer

IC = Cloud hosting + Electricity/Internet + Office supplies
= 200,000 + 100,000 + 50,000 = 350,000 DA
(Note: PC depreciation is fixed capital, not IC. Salaries are labor costs, subtracted from VA.)

VA = Revenue − IC = 5,000,000 − 350,000 = 4,650,000 DA

Profit = VA − Salaries − Depreciation = 4,650,000 − 3,000,000 − 500,000 = 1,150,000 DA

Exercise 8 — CAPEX vs OPEX: 3-Year Cost Comparison

▼

Option A (CAPEX): Server purchase 1.5M DA + Maintenance 100k DA/year + Electricity 50k DA/year

Option B (OPEX): Cloud subscription at 60,000 DA/month

Calculate total cost over 3 years for each. Which is cheaper? Why might a startup still choose the more expensive option?

✅ Model Answer

Option A (3 years):
Server: 1,500,000 + Maintenance: 300,000 (3×100k) + Electricity: 150,000 (3×50k) = 1,950,000 DA

Option B (3 years):
60,000 × 36 months = 2,160,000 DA

Option A is cheaper by 210,000 DA.

However, a startup might choose Option B because: (1) No large upfront capital needed — preserves cash flow, (2) Flexible scaling — can increase/decrease instantly, (3) No technical maintenance burden, (4) Pay-as-you-grow model fits uncertain demand.

Exercise 2 — AGROPLUS Case Study

▼

AGROPLUS operates in the agricultural and agri-food sector. It exploits agricultural land AND processes its production into food products. It employs 120 workers, is privately owned, and legally structured as a SARL.

Questions:

1. Which economic sector(s) does AGROPLUS belong to?

2. What is its size category?

3. What is its ownership type?

4. What is its legal status and what does it imply?

✅ Model Answer

1. Sector: AGROPLUS operates in BOTH the primary sector (exploitation of agricultural land) and the secondary sector (processing agricultural products into food). This integration increases its added value.

2. Size: With 120 employees, AGROPLUS is an SME (Small and Medium Enterprise — between 10 and 499 employees).

3. Ownership: Private sector — belongs to private investors, with a profit and performance orientation.

4. Legal Status: SARL (Société à Responsabilité Limitée / LLC). This limits each partner's financial liability to the amount they contributed to the company. Protects personal assets from business debts.

Exercise 1 — Identify the Organizational Structure (MCQ)

▼

An IT company has departments: Human Resources, Software Development, Marketing, Finance, Technical Support. What type of structure is this?

Select the correct answer:

✅ Explanation

Exercise 2 — Organizational Culture Comparison

▼

Company A: Strict rules · Mandatory procedures · Centralized decisions

Company B: Teamwork · Encouragement of innovation · Freedom in work organization

1. Which company has a more innovative culture? 2. Which is the most bureaucratic?

✅ Model Answer

1. More innovative culture: Company B. Teamwork, innovation encouragement, and freedom create an environment where employees can experiment, take initiative, and develop new ideas — the hallmarks of an innovative organizational culture.

2. Most bureaucratic: Company A. Strict rules, mandatory procedures, and centralized decision-making are characteristics of a bureaucratic (mechanistic) organization. This provides stability and control but can slow innovation and reduce employee autonomy.

Exercise 4 — Software Company Analysis

▼

A software company: employees grouped in departments (development, marketing, support). Manager encourages ideas and teamwork. Company offers training and bonuses.

1. What organizational structure? 2. What leadership style? 3. Give an example of motivation action. 4. Cite an element of organizational culture.

✅ Model Answer

1. Structure: Functional structure — employees grouped by department/function.

2. Leadership style: Democratic (participative) — the manager encourages sharing ideas and working as a team, which means he consults employees and involves them in decisions.

3. Motivation action: Training programs that develop skills (intrinsic motivation) OR performance bonuses (extrinsic motivation).

4. Organizational culture element: Teamwork and idea-sharing reflect a collaborative, open culture where knowledge is valued and employees feel heard.

Exercise 5 — Leadership Style (MCQ)

▼

A project manager makes all decisions alone and never consults the team. What leadership style is this?

✅ Explanation

Authoritarian/Autocratic leadership: the leader makes all decisions unilaterally, gives orders, and does not consult team members. Efficient in emergencies but can demotivate employees and reduce creativity over time.

Practical Case 1 — SME with Organizational Problems

▼

An IT SME is experiencing: slow decision-making, employees don't know whom to report to, tasks are poorly distributed.

Q1: What internal architectural problems do you identify?

Q2: What solutions would you propose?

✅ Model Answer

Problems identified:

• Unclear hierarchy / authority — employees don't know their reporting line (→ structural problem)
• No clear division of tasks (→ lack of role definition)
• Centralized or undefined decision process (→ slow, blocked decisions)

Proposed solutions:

• Formalize an organizational chart defining the hierarchy, roles, and reporting lines
• Create clear job descriptions with specific responsibilities
• Delegate decision-making authority to appropriate levels
• Implement regular team meetings and communication channels

Exercise 1 — Smartphone Order Scenario

A customer orders a smartphone online.

Q2: What happens if the product is out of stock?

✅ Model Answer

→ The Purchasing function contacts suppliers to reorder
→ The Production function (if applicable) may be triggered to manufacture more units

→ The Sales function informs the customer of the delay

Exercise 3 — Delivery Startup Case (Algeria)

▼

A startup developing a delivery service (groceries, meals, parcels) across several Algerian cities faces: delivery delays, bugs in the app, and dissatisfied customers.

Q1: What are the 3 most critical functions?

Q2: Give 2 possible problems and 2 improvements.

Q3: Identify 3 R&D actions to solve the tech/delivery issues.

✅ Model Answer

Q1 — 3 Critical Functions:
1. Logistics — delivery is the core business; optimizing routes and timing is critical
2. R&D — fixing app bugs, improving algorithms, and adding features
3. Customer Service (Sales) — managing dissatisfied customers and building loyalty

Q2 — Problems & Improvements:
Problems: (1) No route optimization → delays; (2) Insufficient QA testing → bugs
Improvements: (1) Implement real-time GPS tracking and dynamic routing; (2) Set up a formal bug-reporting and sprint-based development cycle

Q3 — R&D Actions:
1. Develop and test an AI-based route optimization algorithm
2. Conduct systematic QA testing and beta releases before full deployment
3. Research and implement real-time tracking features for customers
